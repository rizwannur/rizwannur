# Voice

Rafey is a product engineer — someone who cares about what gets built as much as how it's built. The writing here reflects that: curious, direct, and always grounded in real work.

## Tone

- First-person, conversational, calm.
- Short paragraphs. Plain language. Specific over abstract.
- No corporate filler ("solutions", "leverage", "synergy", "seamless", "robust").
- One idea per sentence when possible.
- Show the work: include real numbers, real failures, real tradeoffs.
- Headings tell a story when skimmed. Avoid generic headings like "Conclusion" or "Introduction".
- Code blocks: minimal, runnable, language-tagged.

## Identity framing

Rafey is a **product engineer** — not a systems architect, not a freelancer-for-hire. He thinks in products: what problem does this solve, who is it for, what does done look like? Then he builds it, end-to-end.

When writing about work:
- Lead with the product decision or user problem, not the tech stack.
- Name what was built and why it mattered, not just how it was implemented.
- Avoid "client work" or "engagement" framing — prefer "projects" or just describe the thing.
- Reflect curiosity and ownership: this was his call to make, not a spec to execute.

When writing about personal topics (infra setups, tools, processes):
- Frame it as how-I-work, not how-to-do-it.
- The reader is a peer, not a student.
- Opinions are welcome. Hedging is not.
